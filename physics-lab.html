🎯 Crafting the Research Question (RQ)

The Variable-Link Strategy

A high-scoring RQ must be specific and focused. Use this template:

"To what extent does [Independent Variable] affect [Dependent Variable] when [Key Control Variable] is held constant?"
  • Bad: "How does light affect resistance?"
  • Good: "How does the intensity of incident light ($I$) from a halogen source affect the resistance ($R$) of a Light Dependent Resistor (LDR) measured at 20°C?"
📊 Managing Uncertainties & Errors

The Propagation Protocol

IB Physics doesn't just want data; it wants to know how much you trust it.

1. Absolute vs. Percentage

Always state the precision of your instruments (e.g., $\pm 0.01\text{ mm}$ for a micrometer).

2. Propagation Rules:

  • Addition/Subtraction: Add absolute uncertainties.
  • Multiplication/Division: Add percentage uncertainties.
  • Powers ($x^n$): Multiply the percentage uncertainty by $n$.
Pro Tip: If your final calculated value has a smaller uncertainty than your instrument's raw precision, something is wrong!
📈 Data Analysis & Graphing

Linearization Strategy

If your raw data is a curve (e.g., $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$), you must linearize it to find a gradient that means something.

Relationship x-axis y-axis Gradient represents...
$y = ax^2$ $x^2$ $y$ $a$
$y = a/x$ $1/x$ $y$ $a$

Error Bars: Ensure your graph includes both vertical and horizontal error bars, and a **Line of Best Fit** vs. **Max/Min Slopes**.

🔍 Evaluation: Random vs. Systematic Error

The "Source-Impact-Improvement" Loop

Don't just list errors. Analyze them using this 3-step loop:

  1. Identify: "There was a parallax error when reading the voltmeter."
  2. Impact: "This is a random error which increased the scatter of my data points, making the $R^2$ value lower."
  3. Improvement: "Use a digital multimeter or a high-speed camera to record readings at eye-level."
Systematic Error Check: If your graph has a non-zero y-intercept when the theory says it should pass through (0,0), you have a systematic error!
