🎓 IB TOK Exhibition Strategy Toolkit

Interactive Guidance for Crafting High-Scoring IA Responses

❓ Choosing & Testing a Knowledge Question (KQ)

The Resonance Test

Ensure your chosen prompt (1–35) works with your specific interests:

  • 1. Object Availability: Can you find a specific, real-world object that actually exists to anchor this question?
  • 2. Complexity Check: Does this KQ allow you to explore a "tension" (e.g., where knowledge is helpful but also limited)? Avoid prompts where the answer is an obvious "yes."
  • 3. Knowledge Lenses: Does the KQ allow you to discuss TOK themes like Power, Responsibility, Culture, or Certainty?
✓ Test Your Choice: Can you explain in one sentence WHY this KQ matters to a specific community of knowers?
📖 Defining Concepts (The Foundation)

Concept Unpacking Routine

For every major term in your KQ, you must move beyond the dictionary:

  1. Step 1: The TOK Definition: How is this term understood within Theory of Knowledge? (e.g., "Evidence" vs. "Opinion").
  2. Step 2: The Interpretation: How might the definition change depending on the Area of Knowledge? (e.g., "Truth" in Mathematics vs. "Truth" in History).
  3. Step 3: Analytical Lenses: Choose 1-2 CONCEPTS (Evidence, Values, Perspective) to deepen the definition.
Example: If your KQ asks about "Bias," don't just say it's "prejudice." Define it as a methodological framework that prioritizes certain perspectives over others.
✓ Quality Check: Have you avoided "dictionary dumping"? Is the definition tailored to your specific argument?
🌍 Identifying Real-World Contexts

The "So What?" Framework

Your object must exist in a specific "real-world context." Define it using these steps:

  • Step 1: Specificity: Avoid broad categories like "Medicine." Use "The 2021 WHO vaccine distribution guidelines."
  • Step 2: Stakeholders: Who is impacted by how knowledge is handled in this context?
  • Step 3: Consequences: What happens if the knowledge in this context is misinterpreted or lost?

💡 Building Arguments (CREL Structure)

The CREL Structure

Use this for each of your three objects to ensure logical flow:

  • C = CLAIM: A direct, one-sentence "answer" to your KQ based on this object.
  • R = REASONING: The "Why." Explain the TOK theory that makes your claim true from that perspective.
  • E = EVIDENCE: Reference specific physical or digital features of your Object.
  • L = LINK: Explicitly state how the object proves your claim.
✓ Quality Check: If you removed the object description, would the argument still make sense? (If so, you need more object-specific evidence!)
✍️ Writing the 300-Word Response (The Blueprint)

The 3-Paragraph Flow

Para 1: The Object & KQ (75-100 words)
Introduce the object. State exactly where it is from. Connect it immediately to the KQ. "This [Object] represents the tension in [KQ] because..."

Para 2: The Analysis (125-150 words)
Develop your argument. Don't just describe the object; analyze its significance. Use TOK terminology (e.g., Justification, Objectivity, TOK CONCEPTS). "The specific feature of [X] on this object demonstrates that..."

Para 3: Justification & Implications (100-125 words)
Why this object? What does it show that another object couldn't? What are the broader implications for knowledge? "Without this object, we would fail to see how [KQ] affects..."

✅ Final Quality Checklist (Before Submission)

Self-Audit

  • [ ] Object Specificity: Is it a specific object (e.g., "The tweet by X on date Y") rather than a generic one (e.g., "Social Media")?
  • [ ] KQ Connection: Does the response answer the prompt, or did I get distracted by the object's history?
  • [ ] Word Count: Am I under the 950-word total limit (approx 315-330 per object)?
  • [ ] Evidence: Is the object used as proof, not just an illustration?
🧠 Metacognitive Checkpoints

Monitoring Your Thinking

The "Stuck" Test: Can you explain your argument to someone who doesn't know TOK? If they are confused, your reasoning is likely too vague.

🚨 Warning Signs: Using "This is interesting" or "This relates to..." without explaining how. These are "filler" phrases that signal a lack of depth.
